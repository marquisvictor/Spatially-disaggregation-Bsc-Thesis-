what is spatial disaggregation ?

spatial disaggregation is the process of separating an entity into parts based on some implicit spatial patterns or relationships (Moore, 2015).

Near accurate population data at micro-levels are essential for the:

  • planning and delivery of services
  • Election preparation
  • Estimation of populations at risk of hazards or infectious diseases
  • Disaster relief operations amongst other use cases
  • Determining housing needs
  • Estimating the number of facilities and amenities needed such as health care, transportation, schools, markets et cetera

recent advancements in compute power are presenting opportunities for alternative approaches to producing reliable, spatially refined estimates of the human population

This paper aims to develop a framework that can be used to determine the population estimates of a geographical micro-level administrative boundary using spatially disaggregated population estimates of a larger region.

Background Literature

According to Eurostat (2010), a population census is a series of processes that involve the total collection, compilation, evaluation, analysis, and release of demographic, economic, and social data pertaining to people living within a specific geographic location

The GIS-based Approach

  • A more digital approach to getting very near-accurate estimates of the population figure

  • It was developed by motivated researchers in cases where inaccessibility or insecurity issues do not allow for census data to be collected, or in situations whereby the last census conducted has become outdated

Steps in English

  • Firstly, a micro-census is collected at randomly selected areas in the region.

  • Then geospatial covariate datasets of areas that do not have a microcensus are obtained. These includes data on: settlement types, school density, gridded household size and the World Pop global gridded population estimates. Most of these geospatial covariate datasets are remotely sensed. That’s where remote sensing come in.

  • The micro-census plus the geospatial covariate datasets are then fed to a machine learning model to extrapolate population estimates for areas that do not yet have a micro-census.

Conclusion

This study has shown that near-accurate population estimates can be generated using state-of-the art remote sensing methodologies.

QGIS, Python programming language, and machine learning techniques were used for spatial and raster analysis, data visualization, and zonal statistics.

The study provides urban planners, researchers and decision-makers access to high-quality population estimates which can be aggregated at any level of granularity
